# Finding what you need to solve problems

**Summary:** Before you can fix a situation, you often need **something you do not yet have in hand**: a **fact**, a **tool**, a **person**, a **document**, or a **physical object**. The umbrella topic is **how we search, remember, and retrieve** those things—at **work**, at **home**, and in **public life**. This page names the pattern and points to common subtopics.

---

## The pattern

A “problem” is rarely only one step. Usually it chains:

1. **Notice** something is wrong or unfinished.
2. **Frame** what “solved” would look like.
3. **Discover** what is missing (skill, part, permission, number, name).
4. **Locate** that missing piece—**in memory**, **in a house**, **online**, **in a shop**, **through a person**.

Step 4 is its own family of problems. Getting better at it reduces **stalling**, **duplicate buying**, and **giving up** because you cannot find the **one** thing that would unlock the rest.

---

## Subtopics (examples)

These look different on the surface but share structure: **reduce search space**, **stable places**, **labels**, **habits**, and **systems** that fail gracefully when you are tired.

| Area | What “finding what you need” often means |
|------|------------------------------------------|
| **Home / physical** | Where are the **batteries**, **manuals**, **spare keys**, **seasonal gear**, **medicine**, **tools**? Same drawer problem as [general-purpose vs specialised tools](general_purpose.md)—but here the cost is **time and stress**, not only money. |
| **Information** | Which **procedure** applies? What **number** or **password** is correct? Who **owns** this decision? Search is part **memory**, part **filing**, part **asking the right question**. |
| **Time and people** | Who has **authority**, **skill**, or **history** with this? “Finding” may mean **getting the right person on the line** or **remembering you already asked**. |
| **Money and risk** | Do I need **insurance**, **a permit**, **a second opinion**? The missing piece is sometimes a **constraint** you have not named yet. |

You do not need one grand system for all of these. You need **recognition**: many daily jams are **retrieval** problems in disguise.

---

## A useful default: fewer hiding places

Across domains, a practical principle matches the kitchen picture in [general-purpose tools vs many specialised tools](general_purpose.md): **a small number of well-chosen places** beats **many ad-hoc piles**. “Everything in its category, categories in a few rooms” beats “every item invents a new rule.” The same idea applies to **digital folders**, **toolboxes**, and **who you ask first**.

## Takeaway

**Finding what you need** is not a single trick; it is a **cross-cutting skill**: **reducing search space**, **making retrieval kind to your future self**, and **noticing** when the real block is “I do not have the missing piece yet”—including **the name of what to look for**. Houses are one vivid place this shows up; the same structure appears in **information**, **relationships**, and **planning**.
