# Example: order follow-up as a bottleneck (Audiophile)

This page is a **worked example** of a [bottleneck](bottleneck.md) in a **business** where **communication and ownership**—not lack of demand—limit how fast **value turns into completed orders and cash**. It ties to [**theory of constraints**](theory_of_constraints.md): the system cannot move faster than its **weakest handoff**.

---

## The symptom

**Audiophile** (a real business in this story) has persistent trouble **following up on simple questions about orders**. That may sound minor, but it **slows the whole business**: orders do not **complete**, which **delays revenue** and **cash flow**. The constraint is not “we need more leads”; it is “**answers and forward motion** are not reliably getting through.”

---

## Weekends and product knowledge

On **weekends**, the person who takes the order often has **limited product knowledge**. That points to possible **training** gaps, or a **mismatch** between who is **customer-facing** and who **understands the product**—or simply **volume**: thin coverage on busy days stretches whoever is on the desk.

---

## Structure: who quotes, who administers

There may also be a **structural** issue in how work is **divided**. The **installer** is the one who ultimately **provides the quote**, yet there does not appear to be a clear **administrative layer** that can **answer basic questions** or **move orders forward** quickly. That **handoff gap** creates **friction** and **delay**—classic bottleneck behavior: work **queues** behind the few people who can act.

---

## Broader context (small market, many services)

More broadly, the pattern may reflect the **complexity** of running this kind of business on a **small island**. Audiophile serves **many needs**—**audio installations**, **blinds**, and **other services**—which likely require **different knowledge** and **workflows**. **Variability** of demand, **limited staff**, and **unclear division of responsibilities** can **compound** into a single visible problem: **follow-up** that does not keep pace, so **throughput** of the whole system suffers.

---

## Incentives and perspective

It may also be that there is **not enough incentive**—inside the business or for the owners—to **invest** in fixing the system. **Employees** may face **misaligned incentives** (what they are rewarded for may not include fast, thorough follow-up). The business may still produce **enough** return for the **owners**, so **no one** is really examining it from a **bigger** or **long-term throughput** perspective.

**That is okay.** Not every operation is trying to **optimize** the same variable; a bottleneck analysis describes **where flow is limited**, not a moral verdict. The point is to **see** the trade-offs: **stability** and **adequate returns** can coexist with **friction** customers feel on the ground.

---

## What to take from it

Across **training**, **roles**, and **structure**, the same question applies: **where is the constraint**—the step that most limits **orders completing**? Until that is **relieved** (clear ownership for replies, faster paths for simple questions, alignment of front line and expertise), **extra marketing** or **more quotes** may only **deepen the queue** behind the bottleneck.
