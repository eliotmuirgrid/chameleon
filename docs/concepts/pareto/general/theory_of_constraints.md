# Theory of constraints (TOC)

**Summary:** Every system has a **bottleneck**—a **constraint**—that caps how well the whole thing performs. Improve that part first, and the **whole system** moves; polish everything else while the constraint stays stuck, and you gain little.

---

The **Theory of Constraints (TOC)** is the idea that every system has a bottleneck—called a **constraint**—that limits its overall performance. A constraint is simply the part of the system that **slows everything else down** or sets the **maximum output**.

For example, if you are doing laundry and the dryer takes longer than the washer, the **dryer** is the constraint. No matter how fast you wash clothes, everything still waits on the dryer.

---

## Everyday life

This shows up all the time. In a **kitchen**, the constraint might be a single cutting board or the person doing prep—so improving prep flow speeds up the whole meal. In a **morning routine**, the constraint might be the **bathroom**—so staggering usage or preparing things in advance can make everything run more smoothly.

---

## Business

The same idea applies directly to **business**. In a supply chain, imagine a company that can manufacture 1,000 units per day, but **shipping** can only handle 600 units. Shipping becomes the constraint, and the entire business is effectively limited to **600 units per day**. Producing more does not help—it only creates **inventory buildup**. The smart move is to improve or reorganize shipping (more carriers, better scheduling, batching), just as you would optimize the dryer, kitchen prep, or bathroom usage.

---

## The pattern

Across these examples, the pattern is the same: **find the constraint**, **focus effort there**, and the **whole system** improves.
