# Toilet cleaning

Cleaning a toilet is mostly about **removing biofilm, mineral scale, and stains** from **porcelain** (and keeping **hard water** from building up under the rim). It is **not** the same job as [waxing metal](waxing_metal)—different materials and chemistry—but the same habits help: **use the right cleaner for the problem**, **ventilate**, and **read the label**.

## Routine bowl cleaning

1. **Flush** once to wet the bowl.
2. Apply a **toilet bowl cleaner** (liquid or gel) **under the rim** and on the waterline so it coats the bowl above and below the water.
3. Let it sit for the **time on the label** (often a few minutes).
4. **Scrub** with a **toilet brush**, especially under the rim and the trap outlet.
5. **Flush** again. Rinse the brush over the bowl and store it so it can dry.

Weekly or biweekly cleaning prevents rings and keeps odor down. For **general** cleaning, a standard bowl cleaner or **dilute bleach** (only where appropriate—see safety) is usually enough.

## Hard water, rust-colored stains, and tough rings

**Mineral scale** and some stains need **acidic** bowl cleaners (many products marketed for **lime** or **rust**). They work well on porcelain when used **as directed**—do not mix them with **bleach** or other products.

A **pumice stone** made for toilets can lift stubborn rings **wet**, with light pressure; avoid scratching soft or coated surfaces and test a small area if unsure.

## Tank tablets and drop-ins

**Blue tablets** or **chlorine tablets** that sit in the **tank** are convenient but can **speed wear** on rubber **flappers and seals** over time. Many manufacturers recommend **bowl cleaners** applied to the bowl instead of continuous tank dosing. If you use tank products, **inspect** the flapper periodically.

## Safety

- **Never mix** bleach (chlorine) with **acid** toilet bowl cleaners or **ammonia**—reactions can release **toxic gas**.
- **Ventilate** the bathroom when using strong cleaners.
- **Wear gloves**; keep products away from children and pets.
