## Human impact during COVID-19

I had a manic episode during COVID-19 and lost connection with my children.

That personal loss did not happen in isolation. It happened inside systems that were already overloaded and too rigid to adapt quickly.

When hospitals and care environments are designed as closed systems, surge capacity becomes hard. Beds, equipment, workflows, and staffing pathways cannot be reconfigured fast enough. Mental health support then competes with urgent physical care in a structure that has little room to flex.

In practice, this can mean delayed access, fragmented communication, shorter or more transactional interventions, and fewer opportunities for continuity of care when a person is most vulnerable.

For families, the consequences can be severe: separation, confusion, fear, and relationship breakdown that can persist long after the crisis peak ends.

The design takeaway is that infrastructure choices are not neutral. Inflexible healthcare systems can amplify human suffering, while more modular and adaptable systems can protect both clinical outcomes and family stability under stress.
