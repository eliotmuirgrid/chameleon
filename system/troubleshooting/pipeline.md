# Pipeline

A pipeline is like water moving through a set of pipes. The water starts at one end and flows through each section until it reaches the outlet. Each part depends on the one before it. If the water stops anywhere, everything after that point stops too.

Imagine you turn on a hose and no water comes out. You don’t panic or replace the whole system. You start checking, step by step, where the flow might have stopped. 



Start at the beginning.  Is the tap switched on?

Then look at the hose. Is it kinked or blocked? If that’s fine, you move backward to the valve. Is it open? 

Then check the outlet - does it have controls which are needed to open it?

Once you fix that one issue, the water usually starts flowing again through the whole system.

This is why thinking in pipelines is useful. When something isn’t working, you don’t try to fix everything at once. You just ask a simple question: where did the flow stop? Then you focus only on that spot.

Even if real systems are more complicated, this way of thinking keeps things clear. You follow the flow, find the break, and fix it.

This same idea applies to many many other physical and non physical systems. When you need a **quick** fix at one step before you clean up the whole flow, that is [immediate solutions](../design/immediate_solutions.md).
