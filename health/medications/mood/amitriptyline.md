# Amitriptyline

## What it is

Amitriptyline is an older antidepressant medicine in the tricyclic antidepressant (TCA) group.

It is now often used at lower doses for chronic pain, migraine prevention, nerve pain, and sleep support, not only for depression.

## How we think it might work

The current model is that amitriptyline increases signaling of serotonin and norepinephrine in the nervous system.

It also affects histamine, acetylcholine, and sodium channel activity, which may explain:

- pain-calming effects,
- sedation/sleep effects,
- and side effects like dry mouth or constipation.

## Typical dosage ranges (general)

Dosage depends on the condition, age, and side-effect tolerance.

Common adult ranges used in practice:

- Pain, migraine, sleep support: often starts at `10-25 mg` at night, then may increase slowly (often `25-75 mg`, sometimes up to around `100 mg`).
- Depression: often starts around `25-50 mg/day`, then may titrate higher (commonly `75-150 mg/day`, sometimes higher under specialist care).

Lower starting doses are common in older adults.

## Known negative side effects

Common side effects:

- sleepiness or fatigue (especially early in treatment),
- dry mouth,
- constipation,
- blurred vision,
- dizziness (including when standing),
- weight gain or increased appetite,
- sweating,
- trouble urinating.

Possible sexual side effects:

- Yes, it can affect sexual drive.
- Some people report lower libido.
- Some people report delayed orgasm or trouble with arousal/erection.

Serious side effects that need urgent medical advice:

- fainting, severe dizziness, or irregular heartbeat,
- confusion, severe agitation, or new suicidal thoughts,
- severe constipation/urinary retention,
- signs of serotonin toxicity when combined with other serotonergic drugs.

## Important safety notes

- This is general information, not a personal treatment plan.
- Dosing should be set by a licensed clinician.
- Amitriptyline can interact with many medicines and can be dangerous in overdose.
