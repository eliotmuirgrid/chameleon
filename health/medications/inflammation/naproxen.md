# Naproxen

## What it is

Naproxen is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) used to reduce pain and inflammation.

It is commonly used for musculoskeletal pain, menstrual pain, arthritis flares, and some headache syndromes.

## How we think it might work

Naproxen inhibits cyclooxygenase (`COX`) enzymes and lowers prostaglandin production.

This helps reduce inflammation, pain signaling, and associated swelling.

## Typical dosage ranges (general)

Dose depends on diagnosis, product type, and local prescribing guidance.

Common adult oral ranges in practice:

- Often `250-500 mg` per dose.
- Usually once or twice daily, depending on severity and clinician advice.

Practical timing notes:

- Usually taken with food (or after food) to reduce stomach irritation.
- Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest appropriate duration.

Maximum daily dose and duration should follow the exact product label and clinician instructions.

## Known negative side effects

Common side effects:

- stomach upset, heartburn, abdominal pain, nausea,
- dizziness or headache.

Important risks:

- gastritis, ulcer, or gastrointestinal bleeding,
- fluid retention and increased blood pressure,
- kidney stress (especially with dehydration or kidney disease),
- cardiovascular risk with prolonged/high-dose NSAID use.

Urgent warning symptoms:

- black stools, vomiting blood, or severe stomach pain,
- chest pain, shortness of breath, severe dizziness, or fainting,
- facial swelling, wheeze, or other signs of allergic reaction.

## Important safety notes

- This is general information, not a personal treatment plan.
- Dosing should be set by a licensed clinician.
- Avoid combining with other NSAIDs unless explicitly advised.
- Check interactions carefully, including anticoagulants and some blood pressure medicines.
