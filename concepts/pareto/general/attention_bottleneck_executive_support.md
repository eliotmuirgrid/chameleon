# Attention as a bottleneck and executive support

**Summary:** Sometimes the **constraint** is not a slow process or missing information but **human attention**—**focus** and **bandwidth** are the scarce resource. A **standard response** is to follow a clear **protocol**: bring in **executive-style support** (an **executive assistant** or similar role) to handle work that sits on **broken or heavy external systems**—vendors, bureaucracy, follow-ups, scheduling. **If** those external systems ran **smoothly**, there would be **less need** for such roles; the role exists partly because **friction** in the environment is real.

---

## The bottleneck

When the limiting factor is **focus and attention**, you cannot **think harder** or **work longer** forever without hitting a ceiling. The **bottleneck** is **cognitive and temporal**: there are only so many hours and so much **quality attention** per day. [Theory of constraints](theory_of_constraints.md) still applies: the system (your life or your company) cannot move faster than that **constraint** until something **changes**.

---

## A common protocol

One **standard approach** is to **hire** (or **allocate**) an **executive assistant**—or equivalent support—whose job is to **deal with** external **broken or fragmented systems**: chasing answers, coordinating schedules, navigating paperwork, closing loops that would otherwise **consume** the principal’s attention.

That is not a sign of personal failure; it is **infrastructure** for a world where **many** systems do not run as smoothly as they should.

---

## Why the role exists

**Executive support** scales when **the outside world** does not: if **everything** worked smoothly—reliable vendors, clear channels, instant follow-up—**less** of this work would be needed. The role is **partly a response to friction**; reduce friction everywhere, and the **same** outcome might need **fewer** people in **pure coordination** roles.
