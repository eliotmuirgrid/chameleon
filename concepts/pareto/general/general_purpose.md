# General-purpose tools vs many specialised tools

**Summary:** A **small set of general-purpose** tools—things you reach for again and again—often beats owning **dozens of single-job gadgets**. The general set costs **less**, takes **less space**, **builds skill that transfers**, and **fails you less often** in day-to-day life. The kitchen is an easy place to see why.

---

## A kitchen picture most people recognize

Picture **two** approaches:

| Approach | What it feels like |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Small general set** | One sharp **chef’s knife**, a **cutting board**, a **large bowl**, a **skillet** or **Dutch oven**, **tongs** or a **spatula**, a **sheet pan**. |
| **Many specialised tools** | Avocado slicer, egg separator, dedicated strawberry huller, garlic press that only fits one clove shape, electric gadget for one sandwich, five different “easy” choppers in a drawer. |

Both can live in the same kitchen. The **first** path stays **fast, calm, and tidy**. The **second** path often means **searching drawers**, **cleaning odd parts**, and **forgetting** you own the thing until after you needed it.

---

## Why the small general-purpose set is usually more *powerful* in practice

**Power** here does not mean “more features on the box.” It means **you get more useful work done** with **less friction**.

1. **You learn once, apply everywhere**  
   A **chef’s knife** teaches hand position, rocking cuts, and safe control. That skill moves from **onions** to **herbs** to **chocolate** to **trimming meat**. A gadget often teaches **one motion** for **one shape** of food—and then sits in a drawer.

2. **Fewer things to own, store, clean, and replace**  
   One good **mixing bowl** can **mix**, **temporarily hold** prepped veg, **serve** a salad, or **cover** dough. A stack of single-purpose bowls and containers **multiplies** clutter and **decision fatigue** (“which lid went with which?”).

3. **The same tool handles surprise tasks**  
   **Tongs** flip meat in a pan, **toss** salad, **grab** hot pasta from water, and **reach** something on a high shelf. You do not need a **new** tool for every new task—so you stay **unstuck** when dinner changes at the last minute.

4. **Maintenance and durability compound**  
   You might **hone one knife** and know how it behaves. A drawer of cheap gadgets often means **broken clips**, **lost batteries**, **weird nonstick** that flakes, and **no spare parts**.

5. **Money and attention go farther**  
   Buying **one** solid pan and learning **heat control** beats buying **five** thin “special” pans that each do only one job poorly.

## Concrete examples (everyday cooking)

- **Chef’s knife + board** — Chop vegetables, mince garlic (with a little salt or the flat of the blade), slice bread if you must, break down chocolate. No separate “vegetable chopper,” “garlic-only press,” and “bread saw” required for normal nights.
- **One heavy pot (Dutch oven–style)** — Braise meat, simmer soup, boil pasta, bake a round of **no-knead bread** with the lid on. Same vessel, many modes.
- **Sheet pan** — Roast vegetables and meat, bake cookies, catch drips under a pie, toast nuts. One flat rectangle, many jobs.
- **Large mixing bowl** — Whisk eggs, dress a salad for four, hold peeled potatoes, mix dough. The bowl is **boring**—that is the point: it is **flexible**.
- **Tongs** — Turn chicken, serve spaghetti, flip asparagus, pull hot jars from water. **One grip skill**, many situations.

None of these are “clever.” They are **general-purpose**: **broad roles**, **simple shapes**, **repeatable motions**.

---

## Honest limit: when a specialised tool *is* worth it

Sometimes a **narrow** tool is right: **accessibility** (a grip-friendly opener), **real safety** (a thermometer for poultry), or **high volume** in a professional kitchen where seconds matter. The point is not “never specialised.” The point is that **defaulting** to **a small, strong general set** first—then adding **only** special tools that **earn their keep**—usually beats **starting** from a pile of gadgets.

---

## Takeaway

A **small general-purpose** toolkit works like a **language with a small grammar** you master deeply: you can say **many** things. A **closet of specialised** gadgets is more like **memorizing phrases** for exact scenes: you are **helpless** when the scene changes. In the kitchen—and beyond—**general-purpose** tools **pay rent** every week; **specialised** tools often **pay only on rare Tuesdays**.
