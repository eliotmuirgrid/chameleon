# Nutritional tracking realities

## Concept

A useful nutrition tracking system must work on busy, imperfect days, not only on ideal days.

In practice, that means the system must tolerate:

- time pressure,
- substitutions,
- partial logs,
- and family-level constraints.

## Why

Most real days are fragmented by work, kids, travel, and errands.

If the system only works with perfect planning, it fails when life gets normal.

The goal is not perfect data. The goal is enough consistent signal to improve decisions over time.

## Method

Design tracking rules for reality:

1. Log what you can, when you can.
2. Prefer approximate consistency over missing entries.
3. Allow food substitutions without breaking the routine.
4. Keep one or two anchor meals stable.
5. Focus on trend quality, not single-meal precision.

## Example

On a high-friction day (service appointment, management meeting, errands):

- Subrina soup was tracked (`257 g`).
- Breakfast stayed structurally the same.
- Blueberries were swapped for papaya based on availability.

This is a successful day because the system still produced usable data under stress.

## Quick test

Ask:

1. Can we still log under time pressure?
2. Can we substitute ingredients without confusion?
3. Do we still get enough signal for weekly decisions?

If yes, the tracking system is robust enough for real life.
