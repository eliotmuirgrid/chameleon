# Muscle energy at rest and when used

**Summary:** **Skeletal muscle** burns **energy** all the time—not only when you exercise. **At rest** it is part of **daily energy use** and contributes to **basal metabolic rate (BMR)**—the calories you burn just to stay alive, alongside background work in organs and tissue (brain, liver, heart, and so on). **When active**, demand **jumps**, pulling in more **fuel** (including **glucose**). That dual role ties **movement**, **metabolism**, and **blood sugar** together.

---

## Energy at rest

Even when you are **still**, muscle is **metabolically active**: maintaining cells, repairing protein, generating **heat**, keeping **tone**, regulating what cells need day and night—all of that costs **ATP**. Muscle requires **more** energy to sustain than fat, so **more muscle mass** usually means **more** baseline burn at rest (age, hormones, and training matter too). Muscle is not “off” between workouts; it is part of the **steady** cost of running the body.

The extra resting burn from muscle is **easy to overstate** in casual advice. A pound of muscle burns on the order of **6–10 calories per day** at rest, compared with about **2–3** for a pound of fat. Muscle is clearly **more** metabolically active, but the **absolute** bump in daily calorie burn from adding muscle is **modest**.

The **deeper** payoff is **systemic**: muscle helps you **train harder**, improves how your body **partitions nutrients**, and supports **metabolism during dieting**—not only as a passive calorie burner, but as something that makes the **whole** system more capable and efficient.

---

## Energy when used

When muscles **contract**—from a short sprint to a long walk—**energy demand** rises **sharply**. The body supplies **ATP** faster, drawing more on **glucose** and **fat**, and using **oxygen** when activity is sustained. **Recovery** after hard work also uses energy for repair and replenishment.

**Active muscle** can take up **glucose** from the blood with **less reliance on insulin** than resting tissue during and after movement. That is one reason **light activity after a meal** helps **flatten** glucose curves—see [Glucose spikes](nutrition/glucose_spikes.md).

---

## Why the concept matters

- **Glucose and insulin:** Muscles are a **major sink** for glucose; how much you **move** and how much **muscle** you carry shape **day-to-day** fuel use.
- **Energy balance:** Resting use sets a **floor**; **activity** adds **variable** burn—both sides matter for how fuel from food is **spent**.
- **Training:** **Strength** and **endurance** work change how efficiently muscle **uses** energy over time (this note stays conceptual; details belong in sport-science depth elsewhere).
