# Specific praise

**Summary:** **Specific praise** works better than vague compliments because it turns a generic “good job” into **clear feedback**: it names **what** created value, so the other party can **understand**, **trust**, and **repeat** it. That applies to **people** and to **AI**—any system that learns from signals.

---

**Specific praise** is more effective because it turns a vague compliment into **clear feedback**. Instead of “good job,” it shows **exactly what** created value, making it easier to **understand**, **trust**, and **repeat**.

It **reinforces the right behavior** by pointing to the **specific action** that worked. It also tends to feel **more genuine**, because it shows **attention to detail**. Over time, this helps people (and systems) **internalize** what they are doing well and **align** future actions accordingly.

---

## Examples

**Employee:** Instead of “great work,” say: “You took initiative to organize the CEO’s dental checkup and negotiated pricing despite limited insurance—that saved money and reduced friction.”

**Child:** Instead of “good job cleaning,” say: “You sorted your toys and put books back neatly—that makes things easier to find.”

**AI:** Instead of “nice answer,” say: “You broke the idea into clear steps with examples, which made it easy to follow.”

---

## Why it generalizes

In all cases, **specificity reduces ambiguity** and tells the system **exactly what signal to repeat**.
