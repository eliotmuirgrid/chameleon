# USB-C belief change

## Context

I had a wave of emotion and cognitive dissonance while working through the USB-C power idea.

I am sharing it because it connects to how I think and how I operate.

## What triggered it

I saw how much friction and harm can come from fragmented, high-voltage power design.

A flexible, low-voltage model like USB-C can remove whole categories of problems, not just improve them slightly.

That became personal because it connects to a bigger pattern:

- people are often forced to adapt to rigid systems,
- instead of systems adapting to people.

## Broader reflection

Many mental health struggles may be made worse by economic and social systems that only support a narrow band of human states.

The issue is often not the person. The issue is the system they are required to fit into.

This is emotional for me when I think of my children, including Liam struggling before school, and how blame often goes to the child instead of the system.

## Internal effect

This kind of breakthrough feels like restructuring, not just learning.

I felt:

- a strong emotional wave,
- higher energy demand,
- food cravings,
- and a need for rest.

The analogy is similar to training:
muscles rebuild after stress, and thinking can also reorganize after intense insight.

## Support and recovery

Subrina handled this very well.

She noticed this was not a normal-state moment, helped me get what I needed, and gave me space to reset.

That adaptability mattered.

I also noticed practical recovery signals:

- dense food helped,
- and sleep felt necessary for consolidation.

## Takeaway

New thinking has a real cost and a real opportunity.

If people support each other through high-demand moments, we can integrate change better and build systems that reduce harm.
